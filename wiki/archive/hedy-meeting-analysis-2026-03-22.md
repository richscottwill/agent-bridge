## Richard's Communication Profile (from transcript analysis)

### Strengths

1. **Expert in 1:1 mentoring contexts.** Richard is most articulate and confident when teaching Adi about pacing, ICCP, and analytical frameworks. He explains complex concepts clearly, connects tactical work to strategy, and encourages iterative improvement. These conversations are his highest-quality communication — clear, structured, and genuinely helpful.

2. **Solution-oriented with peers at his level or below.** With Carlos (MX), Richard proactively offers solutions from his EU experience, volunteers to send documentation with screenshots, and drives the agenda. He's warm, efficient, and collaborative. This is Richard at his best — helpful without being overbearing.

3. **Strong cross-market knowledge.** Richard naturally connects dots across markets (AU automotive vertical idea came from hearing about it in AU sync, then suggesting it for MX). He references EU invoice processes to help MX. This cross-pollination is a genuine differentiator.

4. **Good at framing problems politically.** In the Expansion Team discussion with Brandon, Richard identified the political dynamics quickly — who wrote the doc, what the tone implied, which teams were being targeted. He gave Brandon actionable advice: "she needs to reply to Harji's SOP to include the pay search perspective."

### Weaknesses

1. **Disappears in group settings.** This is the biggest signal. In the 42-minute Weekly Team Sync (Brandon, Andrew, Stacy, Richard), Richard spoke 9 times. Andrew spoke 126 times. Stacy spoke 80 times. Brandon spoke 39 times. Richard's contributions were mostly short — "Yep, I am," "It's Apple specifically," "Yeah, I think that's a good idea." He's not asserting his expertise in the room where it matters most for visibility.

2. **Filler and hedging language.** Richard uses a lot of softening language: "I think just like on top of that," "Yeah, just making sure like you, just make sure like we work through like all the monthly invoice," "I don't know, because with branded keywords, what could be the problem?" The hedging ("I think," "I don't know," "kind of like") undermines the authority of his points. Compare this to how he speaks with Adi — direct, clear, no hedging.

3. **Reactive rather than agenda-setting in 1:1s with Brandon.** In the Brandon syncs, Richard provides updates when asked but doesn't drive the strategic narrative. Brandon sets the direction; Richard executes. The Expansion Team conversation is an exception — Richard brought the issue proactively and had a clear recommendation. More of that.

4. **Verbose on operational details, thin on strategic framing.** When Richard talks about budgets, invoices, and POs, he's thorough. When the conversation shifts to strategy (Jasper AI evaluation, testing frameworks, AEO), he's brief — 5 turns in the Jasper meeting, mostly evaluative one-liners. He needs to bring the same depth to strategic conversations that he brings to operational ones.

5. **Doesn't close loops in meetings.** Several sessions show Richard agreeing to follow up ("I'll email it with screenshots," "I'll look into it") but the meeting minutes don't show him summarizing commitments or confirming next steps. Closing with a clear recap of who-does-what would strengthen his presence.

## Improvement Recommendations

### Immediate (next 2 weeks)
1. **Prepare 2-3 talking points before every team sync.** Write them down. Force yourself to deliver them even if the conversation doesn't naturally go there. The goal is to go from 9 turns to 20+ in group settings.
2. **Replace "I think" with declarative statements.** Instead of "I think just like on top of that budget and invoicing issues," say "The budget and invoicing issues with PIM are the main risk here." Practice this in every meeting.
3. **Close every meeting with a recap.** "So to confirm — I'm doing X by Monday, Carlos is handling Y, and we'll reconvene on Z." This is a small habit that signals leadership.

### Medium-term (next month)
4. **Bring a strategic POV to every Brandon 1:1.** Don't just report status. Come with a recommendation, a framework, or a question that shows you're thinking ahead. "Here's what I think we should do about X, and here's why."
5. **Speak up on strategic topics.** In the Jasper meeting, Richard had a good insight about high-volume ad testing but delivered it passively. When you have a strategic opinion, lead with it — don't wait to be asked.
6. **Document your Adi mentoring frameworks.** The way you explain pacing, ICCP, and seasonality proxies to Adi is genuinely good teaching. Write it down as a playbook. That's an artifact that compounds.

### Long-term (quarter)
7. **Build a reputation as the person who frames the problem.** Richard's political read on the Expansion Team doc was sharp. Bring that same framing ability to testing strategy, AEO, and cross-market playbooks. Be the person who names the problem before others see it.
