# Eval B — Blind Subjective Review: Workstreams 3, 4, 5 (v4)

Reviewer: wiki-critic (Eval B, no visibility into Eval A)
Date: 2026-04-05
Method: Kate simulation — 10 minutes per workstream, reading as a senior leader who needs to understand what this team did, why it mattered, and what's next.

---

## Workstream 3: Audiences and Lifecycle

### First Paragraph Test — 8/10
The opening paragraph does real work. It names the dollar outcome ($765K iOPS), identifies the strategic shift (acquisition → lifecycle), and tells you what you'll learn. One weakness: "supports the Testing Approach narrative for Kate" is meta-scaffolding that a reader outside the writing process wouldn't need. That sentence serves the author, not the audience.

### Shareability — 8/10
Kate could forward this to a peer and it would land. The three-phase build is a clean story: plumbing → data integration → net-new channel. The match rate jump (13% → 30%) is a concrete, memorable detail. The F90 section is where shareability dips slightly — the legal dependency language gets dense and the reader has to work to extract the timeline.

### Actionability — 8/10
The doc answers "what happened" and "what's next" clearly. The F90 section gives specific conditions: LiveRamp 1P by end of April → May launch. The risk section names the dependencies without hedging. One gap: the 31.7% → 35.4% target is stated but the mechanism by which Engagement campaigns influence purchasing behavior is asserted, not explained. A reader who needs to defend this target in a review would want one more sentence on the causal logic.

### Signal-to-Noise — 8/10
Tight throughout. The cross-functional partners section is the weakest — it reads as a name-drop list that restates information already embedded in the narrative. If every partner is already introduced in context where their contribution matters, the standalone section is redundant. The AGENT_CONTEXT block is well-structured and earns its place for machine consumers.

### Voice — 8/10
The prose reads like a person explaining work they understand. "Platform limitations are problems to solve, not constraints to accept" is a genuine insight stated plainly. The question-header structure works — each section answers its header. The voice stays consistent and avoids both corporate bloat and false casualness. Minor ding: "This was the plumbing" is a nice touch, but "the foundational audience infrastructure that made everything else possible" immediately after it is the kind of explanatory doubling that dilutes the original phrase.

## Workstream 4: User Experience

### First Paragraph Test — 8/10
Strong opener. Names the two headline results (+13.6K regs, +187% CVR), identifies the problem space (funnel leakage), and tells you what you'll learn. Same meta-scaffolding issue as WS3 — "supports the Testing Approach narrative for Kate" is process language, not reader language. But the rest of the paragraph earns its place.

### Shareability — 9/10
This is the most shareable of the three. The baseline problem is visceral: 85% drop-off at the landing page, 60% at registration start, then complete loss of product context. Any leader who has ever looked at a funnel will feel that. The Gated Guest failure → pivot → in-context success is a genuine learning story, not a sanitized win narrative. The CA results are concrete and the methodology (diagnose → localize → validate) is stated as a repeatable playbook. Kate could hand this to someone outside the team and they'd understand both the problem and the approach.

### Actionability — 7/10
Here's where it softens. The 2026 portfolio section lists seven initiatives across different timelines. The individual descriptions are clear, but the section reads more like a roadmap inventory than a decision framework. A reader finishes knowing what's planned but not how to prioritize if resources get cut, or which initiative has the highest expected value. The compounding argument ("they compound when deployed together") is stated but not quantified — how much does Baloo + Aladdin together deliver versus either alone? The Polaris weblab results are pending (April 6-7), which means the doc is shipping with a known data gap. That's honest, but it means the accuracy of the Polaris claims can't be fully assessed yet.

### Signal-to-Noise — 7/10
The appendices are the issue. Three appendix tables plus a portfolio timeline table — that's four tables in a doc that's already long. The CA test results table (Appendix A) is followed by a good interpretation sentence, so it passes the table-abuse test. But Appendix B (2026 UX portfolio timeline) is a seven-row table that largely restates what the narrative already covers. If you removed Appendix B entirely, the doc loses nothing — every initiative is already explained in the "What does the 2026 UX portfolio look like?" section. Appendix C (Polaris rollout status) is two sentences that could be folded into the Polaris section. The appendix structure creates the impression of thoroughness but actually introduces duplication.

The doc is also the longest of the three by a meaningful margin. The 2026 portfolio section alone is longer than some of the other workstream docs' entire bodies. Economy pressure is warranted.

### Voice — 8/10
The narrative voice is strong. "The landing page experience was the single largest source of funnel leakage — and the team had no direct control over it without cross-functional partnership" is a sentence that conveys both the problem and the constraint in one breath. The Gated Guest section is particularly well-written — it treats a failure as a learning input without being defensive about it. The Baloo/Shopping Ads paragraph is the one place where the voice shifts toward pitch mode ("this is a step-change in channel capability"), but it recovers quickly.

**Ships: NO.** Two dimensions at 7. The doc is close — the core narrative is the strongest of the three — but it's carrying weight it doesn't need. Specific fixes:

1. Delete Appendix B (2026 UX portfolio timeline). Every initiative is already explained in the narrative. The table duplicates without adding.
2. Fold Appendix C (Polaris rollout status) into the Polaris section as one or two sentences. It doesn't need its own appendix.
3. In the 2026 portfolio section, add one sentence on prioritization logic: if only two of these seven ship, which two and why? This converts the roadmap inventory into a decision framework and lifts Actionability.
4. Remove "supports the Testing Approach narrative for Kate" from the opening paragraph. The doc should stand on its own.

These four changes would lift Signal-to-Noise to 8 and Actionability to 8, putting the composite at 8.2.

## Workstream 5: Algorithmic Ads

### First Paragraph Test — 8/10
Efficient opener. Names the formats (Demand Gen, AI Max), the strategic purpose (extend reach beyond keyword campaigns), and what you'll learn. Same meta-scaffolding note as the others. The paragraph does its job.

### Shareability — 8/10
The $0.39 vs. $2.43 CPC comparison is the kind of number that sticks. The Prime Day result (644% ROAS, 12x YoY) is a clean proof point. The AI Max explanation — "office supplies for small company" triggering an AB ad — makes the concept concrete for a non-technical reader. Kate could share this and the recipient would understand the opportunity.

### Actionability — 8/10
The AI Max section is well-structured for decision-making. It names the two specific risks (cannibalization, budget inflation), states the guardrail requirement (detect within first two weeks), and identifies the coordination dependency (Hydra/Retail PS). The conditional logic is clear: Modern Search consolidation must complete before AI Max can launch. A reader knows what needs to happen and in what order.

### Signal-to-Noise — 8/10
The leanest of the three docs. The appendices are short and each one earns its place — Appendix A has an interpretation sentence, Appendix B has an interpretation paragraph, Appendix C is brief and additive. The BSE paragraph in the Demand Gen section is the one place where the doc wanders slightly — it introduces Business Essentials, DG video, and creative team collaboration in quick succession, and the connection to the main Demand Gen narrative is loose. But it's a minor issue.

### Voice — 7/10
The prose is competent but less distinctive than WS3 or WS4. Sentences like "This is not a volume play; it is an efficiency play" do real work. But the AI Max section drifts toward feature-description language — "expands keyword matching beyond exact and phrase match to semantically related queries, dynamically selects landing page experiences based on user intent, and unlocks enhanced audience signals" reads like a product brief, not a strategy narrative. The doc explains what AI Max does but doesn't quite convey why the team is excited about it in the same way WS3 conveys excitement about the lifecycle shift or WS4 conveys the satisfaction of solving the 85% drop-off problem. The voice is informational where the other two are occasionally revelatory.

**Ships: NO.** One dimension at 7. The doc is structurally sound and the leanest of the three, but the voice needs one pass to move from informational to strategic. Specific fixes:

1. Rewrite the AI Max feature-description paragraph ("expands keyword matching beyond exact and phrase match..."). Instead of listing capabilities, frame it as the strategic problem it solves for the team. Something like: "AI Max solves the keyword ceiling problem differently than Demand Gen. Where DG extends reach through visual placements, AI Max extends reach within search itself — showing text ads to users whose queries signal business intent without matching our exact keywords." Then give the example. This reframes the feature list as a strategic narrative.
2. In the Demand Gen section, tighten the BSE paragraph. Either connect it explicitly to the Demand Gen efficiency story (BSE validated that DG economics hold for new product launches) or move it to a brief mention. Currently it reads as an aside.
3. Remove "supports the Testing Approach narrative for Kate" from the opening paragraph.

These changes would lift Voice to 8, putting the composite at 8.0.

## Summary

| Workstream | Composite | Ships? |
|-----------|-----------|--------|
| WS3: Audiences and Lifecycle | 8.0 | YES |
| WS4: User Experience | 7.8 | NO — cut appendix duplication, add prioritization logic |
| WS5: Algorithmic Ads | 7.8 | NO — rewrite AI Max feature paragraph as strategy, tighten BSE aside |

WS3 is the cleanest of the three. It tells one story (acquisition → lifecycle), builds it in three phases, and lands on a clear forward plan with named dependencies. WS4 has the strongest individual narrative (the 85% drop-off → Gated Guest failure → in-context success arc is genuinely compelling) but carries too much appendix weight and lacks prioritization in the 2026 portfolio. WS5 is the leanest structurally but the least distinctive in voice — it reports results accurately without quite making the reader feel the strategic significance.

All three share one cosmetic issue: the "supports the Testing Approach narrative for Kate" sentence in the opening paragraph is meta-scaffolding that should be removed before these ship to anyone outside the writing process.
