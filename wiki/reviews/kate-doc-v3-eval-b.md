## Dimension-by-Dimension

### 1. First Paragraph Test — 9/10

The opening paragraph does four things in four sentences: names the headline result (+35,196 regs, $16.7MM OPS), names the markets, names the three biggest 2026 bets (OCI expansion, Baloo, F90), and closes with the thesis ("every 2026 investment maps to a validated 2025 result"). Kate knows what this document is about and why she should care before she finishes the first paragraph.

Why not 10: The sentence "while building five compounding workstreams now ready to scale worldwide" is slightly vague — "five compounding workstreams" is a claim that only pays off if Kate reads the whole doc. At the opening-paragraph level, the three named bets (OCI, Baloo, F90) are doing the real work. The "five compounding workstreams" framing is structural scaffolding that could be cut without losing the hook.

### 2. Shareability — 8/10

Kate could forward the main body (everything above the appendix) to Todd Heimes with one contextual sentence added at the top. The document is self-contained: it names the team, the results, the methodology, the risks, and the ask. It doesn't assume the reader attended a prior meeting or read a prior doc. The investment summary table at the end is the kind of artifact Todd would actually screenshot for his own review.

Why not 9: Two things would make Kate pause before forwarding. First, the "How We Test" section leads with methodology before results — Todd doesn't care about the four-stage framework, he cares about what it produced. Kate would want to either cut that section or move it to the appendix. Second, the Challenges section names JP at -47.5% and DE missing OP2 — both are real, but Kate might want to frame those differently for Todd depending on the conversation she wants to have. That's a judgment call, not a doc flaw, but it means she can't forward without reading that section carefully.

### 3. Actionability — 7/10

This is the weakest dimension and the one that keeps the doc from being a 9 overall.

Kate finishes reading and knows: the PS team delivered strong 2025 results, has a credible 2026 plan, and the workstreams compound. She understands the risks. She understands the team is small and stretched.

What she does NOT know: What is she being asked to approve? Is this an investment request? A headcount ask? A "keep doing what we're doing" confirmation? The doc implies all three but explicitly asks for none. The investment summary table shows what the team plans to do, but never says "we need X to do it" or "the decision we need from you is Y."

The closest the doc gets is the penultimate paragraph: "The risk of not investing: OCI-eligible markets remain on deprecated Adobe bidding..." This is a good forcing function, but it's framed as risk avoidance rather than a specific ask. Kate leaves the meeting knowing the team is good but not knowing what she's supposed to do about it.

For a doc that's going into an April 16 review, this is a meaningful gap. Kate's 15 minutes should end with a decision, not just understanding.

### 4. Signal-to-Noise — 8/10

The main body is tight. Five workstreams, each with a results paragraph and a 2026 paragraph. The investment summary table is the right capstone — it forces the "2025 signal → 2026 bet" logic into a scannable format. The compounding paragraph after the table is the single best paragraph in the document: it explains why these five things are one strategy, not five projects.

The appendix earns its place. The methodology detail, team roster, and scope descriptions are genuinely useful reference material that would bloat the main body. The decision to separate them was correct.

Two noise issues:

First, the "How We Test" section in the main body partially duplicates the appendix methodology. The main body version mentions the Synthetic Regional Test (82-92% incrementality) and the Gated Guest pivot — both of which reappear in the appendix with more detail. Kate reads the summary version, then hits the detailed version in the appendix, and wonders if she was supposed to skip one. Recommendation: cut the main body "How We Test" to two sentences — "The team follows a four-stage test methodology (appendix). The key validation: 82-92% of Non-Brand registrations are incremental (p<0.001, 2023 SyRT)." — and let the appendix carry the weight.

Second, the Algorithmic Ads section repeats the "644% ROAS" Prime Day stat that already appeared in the Audiences section. Kate notices. It's a small thing, but it signals copy-paste rather than synthesis.

### 5. Voice — 9/10

This sounds like a team that knows what it's doing. Three specific qualities:

The failure handling is confident. "The Gated Guest experiment showed -61% registrations after four weeks — the team paused, diagnosed the failure, and pivoted to in-context registration." No hedging, no blame, no euphemism. The pivot delivered +13.6K regs. That's the story of a team that tests honestly.

The competitive framing is mature. "Walmart Business has driven Brand CPA from ~$40 to $65-77, and we absorb that increase through NB efficiency gains from OCI rather than escalating bids." This is not a team asking for air cover. This is a team explaining how they already solved the problem.

The team section is understated and effective. "Seven-person team delivers five concurrent strategic workstreams across 10 markets while maintaining 25-30 hours per week of operational work." Kate does the math herself. That's more persuasive than any explicit headcount ask.

Why not 10: The document occasionally slips into list-of-facts mode rather than narrative mode. The Modern Search section, for example, moves from the Sole Proprietor insight to the UK Phase 1 test to the 2026 plan in three paragraphs that feel like three separate updates rather than one story. The connective tissue — why the misconception finding led to the specific ad copy that produced the 31% lift — is implied but not stated. A reader who doesn't know the backstory has to infer the causal chain.

## Economy Check (standard sub-rules)

- **Bullet list abuse:** No bullet lists in the main body. Clean pass.
- **Table abuse:** Both tables (OCI results, Investment Summary) have interpretation sentences immediately following. The OCI table is followed by "OCI delivers double-digit registration uplift in every market tested..." and the Investment Summary table is followed by the compounding paragraph. Clean pass.
- **Formatting as content:** The document reads cleanly as plain text. Bold is used sparingly for "Results:" and "2026:" labels within workstream sections. Removing all formatting would not make the document unreadable. Clean pass.
- **Verb check on list items:** The appendix team roster table uses noun phrases ("OCI/Bidding, US performance") rather than verb phrases. This is acceptable for a reference table — these are scope descriptions, not action items. Marginal pass.

## "Kate would..." Summary

Kate would read the whole main body. She'd spend 8 of her 15 minutes on it and feel that was time well spent. She'd understand that the PS team delivered strong 2025 results, has a disciplined methodology, and has a 2026 plan that scales proven bets rather than proposing new ones. She'd note the compounding argument and find it credible.

Kate would pause at the end and ask: "What do you need from me?" The document doesn't answer that question. She'd look for a headcount ask, a budget number, a prioritization decision, or a blocker she can remove — and find none of them stated explicitly. The "risk of not investing" paragraph gestures at urgency but doesn't name the specific investment or approval required.

Kate would forward the main body + investment summary table to Todd. She'd cut the "How We Test" section and add one sentence at the top: "Brandon's team — worth understanding their 2026 plan before we finalize allocations." She'd leave the appendix out of the forward.

Kate would walk into the April 16 review feeling informed but needing the first five minutes of the meeting to establish what decision is on the table. That's a missed opportunity — the document should have told her.

## Verdict: PUBLISH (with one strong recommendation)

Composite 8.2/10. No dimension below 7. This clears the bar.

The one recommendation that would move this from 8.2 to 9+: add a three-sentence "The Ask" section before the investment summary table. Name the specific decision, approval, or resource Kate is being asked to provide. If the ask is "continue current investment and approve the 2026 roadmap as described," say that. If the ask is headcount, say that. If the ask is "remove the Tumble blocker for RoW F90," say that. The document earns the right to make an ask — it just never makes one.
