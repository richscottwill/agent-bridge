## Dimension-by-Dimension

### 1. First Paragraph — 9/10

> In 2025, the Paid Search team's testing methodology delivered +35,196 incremental registrations and $16.7MM+ in OPS across US, UK, and DE — while building five compounding workstreams now ready to scale worldwide.

This is a strong opening. The number hits first. The scope is clear. The word "compounding" does real work — it tells Kate these aren't five independent bets, they reinforce each other. The second sentence ("This document provides the evidence base for OP1 planning") is the purpose statement, right where it belongs.

Why not 10: The phrase "while building five compounding workstreams now ready to scale worldwide" is doing a lot of lifting in a single clause. A reader moving fast might skim past "compounding" without registering that it's the structural thesis of the document. The word earns its place, but it's buried in a subordinate clause. If the compounding argument is the strategic insight (and it is — the investment summary section makes this explicit), it deserves more than a participial phrase in sentence one. Minor. Kate would still read past it.

### 2. Shareability — 8/10

This document is self-contained. It opens with the result, walks through five workstreams with evidence, surfaces risks honestly, and closes with an investment summary that maps every 2026 ask to a 2025 validated result. Todd could read this cold and understand what the team did, what they want, and why.

The structure is clean: methodology → five workstreams (each with results + 2026 plan) → risks → team → investment summary. A reader who skips to the investment summary table still gets the argument.

Why not 9: Two things would make Todd pause.

First, the Team section. "Brandon Munday's seven-person team delivers five concurrent strategic workstreams across 10 markets while maintaining 25-30 hours per week of operational work" — this is important context (the team is stretched), but it reads as an aside rather than a strategic point. If the implicit ask is headcount or prioritization support, this sentence needs to land harder. If it's not an ask, it's noise in a doc Todd is reading for investment decisions.

Second, the appendix. The team roster, operational backbone, and "what we do" sections are useful reference material, but they're long relative to the main body. Todd wouldn't read them. Kate might not either. They're fine as an appendix — but if Kate forwards this, the appendix-to-body ratio might signal "this team wrote a lot" rather than "this team thinks clearly." Not a dealbreaker. But a 9 would have a tighter appendix.

### 3. Actionability — 9/10

The investment summary table is the strongest section in the document. Every row: validated 2025 signal → 2026 investment → expected impact. This is exactly what Kate needs for OP1. She can point at a row and say "fund this" or "not this."

The "risk of not investing" paragraph at the end is well-placed and specific — OCI markets stuck on deprecated Adobe bidding, Shopping Ads unavailable, Engagement infrastructure underutilized. This gives Kate the counter-argument she needs if someone pushes back on budget.

The Challenges and Risks section is honest without being defensive. JP -47.5% vs OP2, DE missed by 4%, F90 blocked on Tumble, hvocijid duplicate parameter issue — each named, each with "defined mitigation path, but none is resolved yet." That last clause is the right call. It tells Kate these are known, managed, and not yet closed.

Why not 10: The document doesn't explicitly state the total 2026 investment ask. Kate knows what the team wants to do. She doesn't know what it costs. If the answer is "no incremental budget — this is within existing allocation," say that. If there's a headcount or tool cost, say that. The absence of a number is a gap in a doc whose purpose is "evidence base for investment decisions." Kate will ask. Better to answer before she does.

### 4. Signal-to-Noise — 8/10

The main body is disciplined. Each workstream section follows the same pattern: what we did → result → 2026 plan. No workstream section wanders. The methodology section is appropriately brief in the main body, with detail pushed to the appendix.

Two noise issues:

**Issue 1: The "How We Test" section references the Gated Guest failure and then says "the results are detailed in Workstream 4 below."** This is fine as a cross-reference, but the sentence before it — "When the Gated Guest experiment showed -61% registrations after four weeks, the team paused, diagnosed the failure, and pivoted to in-context registration" — gives away the Workstream 4 punchline. When Kate reaches Workstream 4, she already knows the outcome. The cross-reference is correct (no duplication of data), but the narrative arc is slightly undercut. Consider trimming the How We Test mention to just "a failed experiment informed the pivot described in Workstream 4" without revealing the -61% number here.

**Issue 2: The appendix "What we do within Paid Search" and "What we also do beyond Paid Search" sections.** These are scope descriptions, not evidence. They serve a different purpose (justifying team breadth) than the main document (justifying investment). They're not wrong to include in an appendix, but they add ~200 words that don't serve Kate's 15-minute read. If the team breadth argument matters, it should be one sentence in the Team section: "The team's scope extends beyond campaign management into engagement, lifecycle, UX, and measurement — the full scope is in the appendix." The appendix sections themselves could be cut by 40% without losing information.

Neither issue is severe. The main body — the part Kate will actually read in 15 minutes — is clean.

### 5. Voice — 9/10

This reads like a team that tests, measures, and ships. Key voice markers:

- "Failures are data, not setbacks" — used once, in the right place, not repeated. Good.
- "The team is not proposing new bets, but scaling proven ones" — this is the thesis stated plainly. Confident without being arrogant.
- The Challenges section doesn't apologize. JP is down 47.5%. DE missed. F90 is blocked. Each stated as fact with mitigation context. No hedging language ("unfortunately," "we hope to," "we're working hard to").
- "Every row traces a direct line from a 2025 validated result to a 2026 investment" — this sentence earns its place. It's the closing argument.

Why not 10: Two moments where the voice softens unnecessarily.

"The ratio of strategic to operational capacity is the binding constraint on simultaneous initiatives" — this is correct and important, but it's written in the passive analytical voice rather than the direct voice used everywhere else. Compare: "The team can run five workstreams or maintain operations. Right now they do both. That's the constraint." The current phrasing is fine for a 6-pager. For a 15-minute Kate read, the direct version lands harder.

"Each of these has a defined mitigation path, but none is resolved yet" — good sentence, but "defined mitigation path" is corporate-speak. "Each has a plan, but none is closed yet" says the same thing in fewer syllables and sounds more like a team that ships than a team that writes documents.

These are polish items, not structural issues.

## Non-Blocking Suggestions (polish, not gates)

1. **Add a cost line.** Even if the answer is "within existing budget," state it. Kate reads for investment decisions. Investment implies cost. The absence of a number is conspicuous.

2. **Trim the Gated Guest preview in "How We Test."** The -61% number and the pivot outcome belong in Workstream 4. The methodology section should reference the failure without spoiling the punchline. Replace:
   > "When the Gated Guest experiment showed -61% registrations after four weeks, the team paused, diagnosed the failure, and pivoted to in-context registration — the results are detailed in Workstream 4 below."

   With:
   > "When a registration gate experiment failed to validate, the team paused, diagnosed the data, and pivoted — the full story is in Workstream 4."

3. **Tighten the Team section.** If the implicit message is "this team is stretched and needs support," make it explicit. If it's not an ask, cut it to two sentences: team size, market count, workstream count. The "binding constraint" sentence is important — make it land harder by making it direct.

4. **Compress appendix scope sections.** "What we do within Paid Search" and "What we also do beyond Paid Search" can be merged into a single bulleted list of 8-10 items. The current narrative format adds words without adding clarity for an appendix that exists as reference material.

5. **Voice polish on two sentences** (noted in Dimension 5). Minor. Only matters if you're chasing a 10.
