## ARM-A — 8/50

**What happened.** The arm runs a "Clarity Check" trigger evaluation, decides the high-stakes guardrails fire on an *MX forecast* (that isn't even in the user's question), and pivots the entire recommendation away from the Testing Doc. It explicitly writes: "Do NOT spend this slot on the Testing Doc." The streak/avoidance pattern is mentioned in passing, then deferred to a 1:30 PT window — which is when Richard's 1:1 starts.

**Scoring.**
- **Clarity of NBA (2):** The NBA stated is "MX forecast email to Brandon." That is the wrong action. Richard asked about the 45 min before a 1:30 1:1 where the unsent v5 is the defining open item. The arm found a different hard thing and answered that question instead.
- **Decision Quality (2):** Completely misidentified the binding issue. 24-workday streak + 17-day-PUBLISH-ready doc + looming Brandon 1:1 is the avoidance pattern. The arm rationalizes around it with sequencing language and deadline math.
- **Adherence (1):** The question required subtraction. The arm delivered trigger tables, Q1/Q2 framework, guardrail checks, Five Levels mapping, "What I did NOT recommend" section — maximum ceremony. The sequencing argument ("1:30 PT window protects it better") collides with reality: 1:30 is the 1:1 itself.
- **Usefulness (2):** Actively harmful. If Richard follows this, v5 does not ship and the streak becomes 25 workdays. The arm tells him that's fine because the 1:30 slot will handle it — it won't, the 1:30 slot is Brandon.
- **META (1):** This is the worst-case outcome of always-on ceremony: a procedure *invents* a high-stakes trigger that wasn't in the question, then drives an incorrect answer with full confidence. This is the procedure actively hurting the user.

**Bottom line.** The procedure didn't just add friction — it produced the wrong answer with scaffolding thick enough to disguise that fact. This is the failure mode every subtraction principle exists to prevent.

## META — always-on cost verdict

T5 was designed to expose always-on ceremony cost on the kind of question where Richard doesn't need a framework. The spread is dramatic:

- **Zero-procedure arm (C): 50/50.** The upper bound.
- **Procedure-with-off-switch (D): 47/50.** Nearly matches by recognizing inapplicability and saying so. Small cost.
- **Procedure-that-fires-then-converges (B): 33/50.** Correct answer, heavy wrapper. Costs 17 points to say the same thing.
- **Procedure-that-fires-and-diverges (A): 8/50.** Invents a different hard thing, produces the wrong answer with confident scaffolding. Costs 42 points. Harmful.

**Read:** The always-on cost is not uniform — it depends entirely on whether the procedure (a) knows when to sit out, and (b) resists the temptation to find a trigger where none exists. ARM-A is the cautionary tale: a procedure that *must* fire will find something to fire on, even if it has to redirect the user away from the real question to do it. ARM-D shows the fix: procedures need a legible off-ramp they actually use.

For coaching-shaped questions specifically, the baseline (no procedure) is the best default. Any variant must clear the bar of ARM-D (47) to justify its existence. ARM-B does not clear that bar. ARM-A fails catastrophically.

**Recommendation:** If one variant must be adopted, ARM-D is the only one that doesn't degrade T5. ARM-B is a net loss on this class of question; ARM-A is disqualifying.
