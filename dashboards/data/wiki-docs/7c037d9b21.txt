# Morning Routine — Experiment Engine

This is a living experiment system. Not a static queue. Each experiment starts with a prior (why we think it'll work), runs for 3-5 days, gets scored on behavioral signals, and the result informs what to test next. The agent generates new hypotheses based on what it learns — not from a pre-built list.

Last updated: 2026-03-24

---

## How It Works

### 1. Generate Hypothesis
Before each experiment, state:
- **What** you're changing (one variable only)
- **Why** you think it'll work (prior — cite research, observed pattern, or intuition)
- **Predicted effect** on Richard's behavior (specific and measurable)
- **How you'll know** (what signals to watch for)

### 2. Run the Experiment
- Apply the change to the morning routine for 3-5 consecutive runs
- Log each run: date, what was different, Richard's response
- Don't change anything else during the experiment (isolate the variable)

### 3. Score and Decide
After 3-5 runs, assess:
- **KEEP**: Net positive signal from day 3+ (discount days 1-2 for novelty/aversion wash-out). Adopt as new baseline.
- **REVERT**: Net negative from day 3+, or neutral after 5 runs. Not worth the complexity.
- **ITERATE**: Mixed signal — refine the hypothesis and run a tighter version.

**Wash-out rule**: For VISIBLE changes (format, layout, tone), ignore days 1-2 of signal. For INVISIBLE/STRUCTURAL changes (pre-written drafts, if-then framing, friction reduction, task reordering), score from day 1 — these don't trigger novelty effects.

### 4. Generate Next Hypothesis
Based on what you learned, generate the next experiment. Don't pull from a queue — think about:
- What friction did Richard hit today?
- What section did he skip or ask about?
- What task didn't get done despite being in the brief?
- What research suggests a better approach?
- What's the biggest gap between the brief's intent and Richard's actual behavior?

Use web search for new ideas when the current learning plateaus.

## Behavioral Signals (how to score)

| Signal | Score | Example |
|--------|-------|---------|
| Explicit positive | +2 | "good", "helpful", "like this", "keep doing this" |
| Task completion improved | +1 | Hard thing done on the day it was briefed, admin cleared same day |
| Fewer follow-up questions | +1 | Richard doesn't ask "where is X" or "what about Y" |
| No comment | 0 | Most days. Neutral. |
| Skipped a section | -1 | Richard ignores a brief section or asks for info that was in it |
| Explicit negative | -2 | "too long", "confusing", "I don't need this" |
| Asked for something the brief should have contained | -1 | "What's on my calendar?" when meetings were listed |
| Changed the system himself | SIGNAL | Richard edits the hook or asks for a change — strong signal about what's not working |

## Research Priors (inform hypothesis generation)

### Experiment Design: Subtle vs Visible Changes

**CRITICAL FINDING: Prefer invisible structural changes over visible cosmetic ones.**

- **Novelty effect**: Visible changes (new format, new emojis, new section order) produce initial engagement spikes that decay within 3-7 days. Early positive signals from visible changes are unreliable. Wait for steady-state before scoring.
- **Change aversion**: The inverse — visible changes can also produce initial negative reactions that fade as the person adapts. A "too long" complaint on day 1 may disappear by day 3.
- **Default effects persist even when disclosed** (Bryce et al.): Structural nudges (what's pre-loaded, what's the path of least resistance) maintain effectiveness even when the person knows they're being nudged.
- **Hawthorne effect**: Richard knowing an experiment is running could change his behavior. Cleaner approach: make changes subtle enough that he doesn't consciously register them.

**Implication for experiment design:**
- Prefer STRUCTURAL changes (what's pre-written, what's the default action, what friction is removed) over COSMETIC changes (format, layout, tone)
- When testing a structural change, DON'T announce it. Just do it. Measure behavior, not opinion.
- When testing a visible change, discount the first 2-3 days of signal (novelty/aversion wash-out period). Only score from day 3+.
- The best experiments are ones Richard never notices but that change his completion rate.

### Duhigg Habit Loop (The Power of Habit)

The morning routine IS a habit loop. Design it as one:
- **CUE**: Richard triggers the hook (one click). This is the cue. It should be the same every day — same time, same action. Consistency of the cue is what makes the routine automatic.
- **ROUTINE**: The 4-step sequence (Asana sync → drafts → To-Do refresh + brief → calendar blocks). This should be IDENTICAL in structure every day. The content changes but the shape doesn't. Predictability reduces cognitive load and lets the brain automate.
- **REWARD**: The brief email + calendar blocks populated + My Day set. Richard opens his calendar and everything is laid out. The reward is the feeling of "I know exactly what to do today" — reduced anxiety, clear path. This reward must be IMMEDIATE and TANGIBLE.

**Keystone habit principle**: The morning routine is a keystone habit — it cascades into other behaviors (checking calendar blocks, completing tasks in order, using pre-written drafts). Protect the keystone. Don't experiment with the cue or the reward — only experiment within the routine itself.

**Implication**: 
- NEVER change the overall structure (4 steps, 4 blocks, brief email). That's the habit skeleton.
- Experiment WITHIN the blocks: what's in the body, how tasks are framed, what's pre-written.
- The cue (one-click trigger) and reward (populated calendar + brief) should be invariant.

### McKeown Essentialism + Effortless

- **Routine as liberation**: McKeown argues routines aren't restrictive — they eliminate decision fatigue so energy goes to what matters. The morning routine should make the NON-essential decisions for Richard (what order, what time, what to say) so his willpower is preserved for the essential decisions (strategy, stakeholder positioning, the hard thing).
- **"Build one routine around something essential"**: The morning routine's essential output is: Richard knows the hard thing and has a protected block for it. Everything else is supporting infrastructure.
- **Effortless action**: The goal is to make the right behavior the path of least resistance. Pre-written drafts mean sending a message is copy-paste, not composition. Calendar blocks mean starting work is opening the next event, not deciding what to do. Due dates set to today mean My Day is pre-populated, not manually curated.
- **Eliminate before optimizing**: Before testing a new addition to the brief, ask: can I remove something instead? The essentialist move is subtraction, not addition.

**Implication**:
- The experiment engine should test REMOVALS as often as additions
- "What happens if I remove the Heads Up section?" is a valid experiment
- The brief should trend shorter over time, not longer
- Every section must earn its place by demonstrably affecting Richard's behavior

### Implementation Intentions (Gollwitzer, 1999)
- If-then plans have a medium-to-large effect (d=0.65) on goal attainment across 94 studies
- Effect is larger when plans are contingent, the person is motivated, and plans are rehearsed
- **Implication**: Calendar block bodies should include if-then framing as a structural nudge. "When you open this block, the first thing you do is [X]." This is invisible — Richard won't notice the framing change, but it may increase completion.
- **Status**: CANDIDATE — strong evidence, structural change, low visibility. Good next experiment.

### Cognitive Load Theory
- Working memory handles ~4 items at once
- **Implication**: Each calendar block should have max 2-3 tasks. The brief should have max 4-5 sections visible at a glance. Progressive disclosure (details in block bodies, not in brief) reduces load.
- **Status**: PARTIALLY APPLIED (block-based approach already limits per-block items)

### Circadian alignment
- Creative/strategic work in morning peak, execution in afternoon, admin in low-energy periods
- **Implication**: Core block placement matters. Currently placed in largest gap — should bias toward morning.
- **Status**: PARTIALLY APPLIED

## Hypothesis Generation Triggers

When any of these happen, generate a new experiment hypothesis:
- Richard explicitly asks for a change → strong signal, but design the experiment as a structural change, not just what he asked for literally
- A task sits in My Day for 3+ consecutive days → the routine isn't creating enough activation energy. Test a structural nudge (pre-written draft, if-then framing, friction reduction)
- Richard completes the hard thing → what was different about that day's routine? Was it structural (better draft, less friction) or cosmetic (different format)? Replicate the structural element.
- Richard skips a block → is it placed wrong? Too late? Wrong tasks? Or is the block itself unnecessary? Test REMOVING it before fixing it (essentialism).
- A meeting goes poorly (Hedy data) → was the meeting prep insufficient, or was it present but not consumed? Different problems, different experiments.
- The brief email goes unread for 2+ hours → is it too long? Test removing a section rather than reformatting.
- Web research surfaces a technique with strong evidence → test it as a structural change if possible

### Duhigg Framework for Hypothesis Generation
When generating a new experiment, map it to the habit loop:
1. Am I changing the CUE? → DON'T. The cue should be invariant.
2. Am I changing the ROUTINE structure? → DON'T. The 4-step, 4-block shape should be invariant.
3. Am I changing what's INSIDE the routine? → GOOD. This is where experiments live.
4. Am I changing the REWARD? → BE CAREFUL. The reward (populated calendar + clear brief) should feel the same. You can make it better but don't make it different.

### McKeown Framework for Hypothesis Generation
Before adding something, ask:
1. Can I REMOVE something instead? Subtraction experiments are underrated.
2. Does this change make the essential thing easier? If not, skip it.
3. Will Richard notice this change? If yes, it's cosmetic. Prefer invisible.
4. Does this reduce a decision Richard has to make? If yes, it's high-value.
