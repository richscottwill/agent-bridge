# Polaris Program

Polaris is the next-generation brand landing page platform for Amazon Business Paid Search, replacing legacy PADESARJAD and MCS pages across all ten markets. Richard is the single point of contact for global Polaris initiatives per Brandon's April 14 direction. This document is the canonical Polaris reference — strategy, market-by-market status, decision log, dependencies, and measurement framework.

## Why Polaris matters

Legacy MCS pages were built market-by-market with inconsistent templates, limited mobile optimization, and no standardized measurement framework. Polaris fixes four problems at once.

First, **consistent template across markets.** One design system, localized per market. Reduces page build time from weeks to days.

Second, **mobile-first design.** Legacy pages were desktop-optimized. Polaris is responsive by default. Mobile traffic is growing across all markets — the April 16 MCS decision confirmed Polaris as the only path forward for paid search LPs.

Third, **weblab-ready measurement.** Polaris pages integrate with Amazon's weblab infrastructure for A/B testing. Legacy pages required manual pre/post analysis that introduced measurement uncertainty.

Fourth, **faster iteration.** Template changes propagate to all markets. A CTA improvement tested in US can roll to EU5 in days, not weeks.

The US MCS brand page template showed a 38bps conversion improvement in early testing. If this holds across markets, the compounding effect across ten markets is material.

## Strategic decisions

On **April 16, 2026**, the MCS team confirmed all paid search landing pages will migrate to Polaris. Legacy PADESARJAD pages are deprecated — no further investment. Mexico is the early test market for structural changes. US and JP are already live on Polaris via weblab.

On **April 14, 2026**, Brandon confirmed Richard as the single point of contact for global Polaris coordination. This closes the gap where market-specific owners were each running their own rollout negotiations with MCS.

## Market priority

Brandon's priority order from March 23, holding: **AU > MX > DE > UK > JP > FR > IT > ES > CA > US-ES**. The sequence reflects market impact, existing Polaris readiness, and the Lena-challenge context in Australia. Each market follows a standard rollout pattern: AEM translation → page build → weblab setup → dial-up → full switch.

Lena's decision to skip the 50/50 phased test is a departure from the team's standard methodology (Decision Principle 4: phased rollout over full migration). ## MX Non-Brand Landing Page Test

The MX Non-Brand landing page test is a distinct Polaris execution thread, separate from the WW brand page rollout documented above. Where the brand rollout follows a weblab-driven measurement path through MCS, the NB test operates outside that infrastructure entirely. This section documents the test framing, method, current status, and dependencies as of May 1, 2026. The reader should understand why MX is the lead market for NB pages, why the measurement method differs from brand pages, and what must be resolved before launch.

### Why MX leads the NB test

Mexico was selected as the early test market for Polaris Non-Brand pages based on two factors: template readiness and strategic sequencing. Vijeth Shetty (MX agency) completed the NB page template and cross-functional coordination by April 1 (Richard, hedy 4/16). Brandon and Richard confirmed MX as the lead market for structural Polaris changes on April 16, consistent with the broader priority order where MX sits second after AU. The initial test targets beauty and auto category NB pages, two verticals with sufficient traffic volume to generate a readable signal within the planned test window (Richard, hedy 4/16).

The AU Polaris LP test provides critical context for MX test design. AU showed a 30% conversion rate drop on Polaris pages, severe enough to trigger a revert (Richard, hedy 4/29). That result reframes the MX NB test: the team now has evidence that Polaris can materially underperform legacy pages, not just produce marginal noise. The MX test is designed with that counterfactual in mind, with conversion rate parity as the minimum success bar rather than an assumed outcome.

### How the test is measured: Google Experiments, not Weblab

The standard Polaris measurement path uses Amazon's weblab infrastructure for A/B testing. NB pages in MX and AU cannot use that path. Dwayne Palmer confirmed that weblab can only be set when the control page routes through MCS (Brandon to Yun-Kang, C065KKT53DJ, 4/24). AU and MX NB pages route users direct-to-registration without an MCS intermediary, making weblab technically infeasible for these flows.

Yun-Kang Chu concurred with the Google Experiments alternative, noting that MX NB data volume is "likely too limited for Weblab conclusion" even if the technical constraint were resolved (Yun-Kang, mpdm-brandoxy-dtpalmer-prichwil, 4/27). Google Experiments provides the A/B split at the ad platform level, routing traffic between the Polaris NB template and the legacy NB page. This is a two-arm test: Polaris NB template versus legacy NB pages. The signals do not indicate additional variant arms beyond these two.

### Success criteria and test window

The primary success metric is conversion rate: Polaris NB pages must achieve parity with or lift over legacy NB pages. The measurement window is two weeks from launch, per the April 16 framing between Richard and Brandon (Richard, hedy 4/16). Given the AU 30% conversion rate drop on brand pages, the team is treating any material CVR degradation as a rollback trigger rather than waiting for extended observation. Confidence level on the two-week window producing a conclusive result is LOW, given MX NB traffic volume constraints that Yun-Kang flagged. If the two-week window is inconclusive, the team will need to extend or supplement with directional analysis.

### Current status (as of May 1, 2026)

The MX NB test is not yet in market. Vijeth Shetty is still working on the MX PS Non-Brand page changes, with a preview URL pending (Vijeth, 5/1). The brand page thread has its own active issues: IT cp/ps-brand Polaris went live April 1, was reverted to legacy on April 16 due to performance issues, and is now parked as it/cp/ps-brand-new under MCS-3004 (Alex VanDerStuyf). That brand page experience reinforces the need for the NB test to launch clean, with known QA issues resolved first.

### What must be resolved before MX NB launch

Three QA issues identified by Yun-Kang Chu on Polaris brand pages also apply to the NB template and must be confirmed resolved before the MX NB test goes live (Yun-Kang, ab-paid-search-global, 4/29). First, subheadlines are not updated on Polaris pages. Second, Paid Search ref tags appear to be overridden on Polaris, which would corrupt attribution data. Third, page load time is longer than usual on Polaris, a factor that directly affects conversion rate and would confound test results.

Beyond QA, the test depends on coordination across three owners. Alex VanDerStuyf owns MX AEM coordination and has confirmed navigation elements. Dwayne Palmer is collecting consolidated brand LP feedback before the weblab/migration path proceeds, per Brandon's directive that all Polaris feedback route through Richard as a single channel (Brandon, ab-paid-search-global, 4/29). Yun-Kang Chu owns the QA resolution for the three issues above. Until the ref-tag override issue is confirmed fixed, launching the NB test would produce unreliable attribution data, making the test results uninterpretable.

## Measurement framework

### Per-market weblab (standard approach)

| Metric | Success threshold | Rollback threshold |
|--------|------------------|-------------------|
| Conversion improvement (CP) | 45% | 35% |
| Registration volume | No degradation | >10% drop for 2+ weeks |
| Bounce rate | Improvement or flat | >20% increase |

The 45% CP success threshold and 35% rollback threshold were established for the US weblab. The same thresholds apply to all markets unless market-specific factors warrant adjustment.

### AU exception (pre/post analysis)

Because Lena directed a full switch without a weblab, AU measurement relies on pre/post comparison:

1. Capture 4 weeks of pre-migration performance (completed)
2. Compare post-migration performance at 2, 4, and 8 weeks
3. Adjust for known confounders: seasonality, promo transitions, CPC trends
4. Report with explicit confidence level (likely MEDIUM due to lack of control)
