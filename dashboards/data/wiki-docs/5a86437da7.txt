# Blind Eval B: OCI Rollout Playbook Rewrite

**Evaluator**: Subjective reader simulation — Brandon Munday (L7) and Kate Rundell (L8)
**Date**: 2026-04-04
**Article**: `shared/context/wiki/staging/oci-rollout-playbook-rewrite.md`
**Prior reviews seen**: None (blind evaluation)

---

## 1. FIRST PARAGRAPH TEST — 8/10

The opening paragraph leads with a result: "35,196 incremental registrations and $16.7MM in OPS across US, UK, and DE." That is the right instinct. Brandon sees a number, sees a dollar figure, and knows immediately what this doc is about and why it matters. The purpose statement is clear — "use it to replicate the rollout in any new market" — and the self-sufficiency claim ("a teammate should be able to follow this doc end-to-end without asking the author") sets the bar for what follows.

What keeps this from a 9: the paragraph ends with two cross-reference links. That is housekeeping, not hook. Brandon does not care about your doc taxonomy in the first paragraph. He cares about the result and the ask. Move the cross-references to the Related section at the bottom, or at most to a brief "companion docs" callout after the first section. The opening should end on the self-sufficiency claim — that is the strongest closing beat.

Brandon would read past it. The dollar figure earns the second paragraph.

## 2. SHAREABILITY — 7/10

This is where the doc starts to strain. Brandon could forward the first three sections (what OCI does, the phased rollout, measurement) to Kate with confidence. The strategic framing is solid: OCI enables an efficiency-over-escalation competitive response, NB CPA improvement absorbs Brand CPA pressure from Walmart. Kate would nod at that framing.

But the doc is too long for a forward. At roughly 3,000 words, this is not a one-pager. Kate is not going to read the MCC structure section. She is not going to read the market-by-market breakdown for all seven markets. She is not going to read the known issues section about duplicate `hvocijid` parameters. If Brandon forwards this, he has to say "read the first half and the decision table at the end" — and the moment he has to add instructions, the shareability drops.

The market-by-market section is the biggest offender. It reads like a status update, not a playbook. CA launched on this date, JP launched on that date, FR has this competitive challenge. That is operational detail that belongs in a market status doc or the Eyes organ, not in a methodology playbook. A playbook should say "here is how to assess market-specific factors" — not enumerate every market's current situation. The current rollout status section has the same problem: it is a snapshot that will be stale in two weeks.

Kate would read the first page. She would skim the decision table. She would not finish the doc.

## 3. ACTIONABILITY — 9/10

This is the doc's strongest dimension. The phased rollout is genuinely executable. Each phase has a clear goal, a duration, entry criteria, exit criteria, and what to watch for. "Run the split for a minimum of four weeks. Compare on registrations, CPA, and ROAS. If lift is below 10% after four weeks, extend two more weeks." That is a recipe someone can follow.

The decision table at the end is excellent. Five situations, five actions, five reasons. A market owner hitting a CPA regression can look at this table and know what to do without calling Richard. That is leverage — Brandon's favorite word.

What prevents a 10: the Phase 1 section is slightly vague on the "how." It says "set up the OCI conversion action, configure the data feed, and trigger test registrations" but does not say where to find the instructions for doing that. The cross-reference to the Execution Guide in the opening paragraph is supposed to cover this, but by the time the reader is in Phase 1, they have forgotten about that link. A single sentence — "For the step-by-step Google Ads setup, see the Execution Guide" — at the end of the Phase 1 section would close the gap.


The measurement section is also strong. The shift from test-versus-control (Phases 2-3) to seasonality-adjusted baselines (Phase 4) is clearly explained, and the reporting format ("OCI lift tracking: W[X] +[Y]% ([Z]% to expectation)") gives people a template they can use immediately.
## 4. SIGNAL-TO-NOISE — 6/10

This is the doc's weakest dimension, and it is what would make Kate put it down.

The signal is strong: phased methodology, measurement framework, validated results, decision guide. That is a tight playbook. But the noise is significant:

The market-by-market section (roughly 500 words) is a status report masquerading as playbook content. "CA launched E2E on March 4, 2026. Landing page optimization is already showing strong results." That is news, not methodology. It will be stale by May. A playbook should describe the pattern for assessing any market — not enumerate the current state of each one. This entire section should be either cut or replaced with a "market assessment checklist" that is durable.

The MCC structure section (roughly 200 words) is infrastructure detail. The insight — "a market joining an existing MCC inherits conversion tracking, which accelerates rollout; a market needing a new MCC requires additional setup time" — is worth one sentence in the Phase 1 section. The MCC IDs and hierarchy belong in the Execution Guide or Eyes, not here.

The current rollout status section (roughly 150 words) is a snapshot. "As of April 2026, seven of ten markets are at full OCI deployment." This is useful today and wrong in three months. It belongs in Eyes, not in a playbook.

The DE test-versus-control reference data table is the strongest evidence in the doc, but it is presented twice — once in the measurement section narrative and once as a standalone table. The table is valuable. The narrative preceding it partially duplicates the validated results section above it.

The Sources section at the bottom is good practice for traceability but adds 10 lines that no human reader will consult. Fine for agent consumption, but it contributes to the length problem.

An L8 with 15 minutes would get value from the first 60% of this doc. The last 40% is operational detail that dilutes the playbook's authority. Kate would wonder why the author did not separate the methodology (durable) from the status (ephemeral).

## 5. VOICE — 8/10

This sounds like Richard. Direct, opinionated where it matters, evidence-first. "The default posture is patience — OCI's learning period means early volatility is expected. Premature intervention is the most common mistake." That is a person with a point of view, not a committee document. "OCI does not fix Brand CPA" in the second section is the kind of direct framing that builds trust — the author is telling you what the tool cannot do before overselling what it can.

The competitive framing is sharp: "When NB CPA drops 50% via OCI, the team can absorb Brand CPA increases from competitors like Walmart without total program CPA degrading. This is the structural foundation of the efficiency-over-escalation competitive strategy." That is strategic thinking embedded in a playbook, which is exactly what makes Richard's docs useful at the L7-L8 level.

What keeps this from a 9: the market-by-market section loses the voice. It shifts from opinionated methodology to neutral status reporting. "FR launched E2E on February 26, 2026, and reached 100% in late March" is factual but voiceless. Compare that to the earlier "Never go from 0% to 100%. Always validate lift before committing more budget." The playbook sections have conviction. The status sections have dates.

## Brandon would...

Read the first half with genuine interest. The phased rollout and measurement framework are exactly the kind of lightweight mechanism he values — a repeatable process that lets a market owner execute without escalating to Richard for every decision. He would bookmark the decision table. He would forward the doc to a new market owner joining the team.

But he would not forward it to Kate as-is. He would say "this is 60% playbook and 40% status update — can you split it?" Brandon reads 20+ docs a week. He can tell when a doc is trying to be two things. The playbook is strong. The status report is fine. They should not be the same document.

He would also notice the length. At 3,000 words, this is a 12-minute read. Brandon would want the playbook portion (methodology, measurement, decision guide) in one doc at roughly 1,800 words, and the market status in Eyes or a separate operational doc.

## Kate would...

Read the first paragraph, nod at the $16.7MM figure, and scan the headers. She would stop at "What OCI does and does not do" — that framing earns her attention because it signals the author is not overselling. She would read the phased rollout headers and the decision table.

She would not read the market-by-market section. She would not read the MCC structure. She would not read the known issues. If she got to the DE test-versus-control table, she would appreciate the data but wonder why it appears twice in different forms.

Her feedback in a doc review would be: "Strong methodology. Cut the status sections — they do not belong in a playbook. The decision table is the best part. Lead with the methodology and the table, put everything else somewhere else. This is a 1,500-word doc hiding inside a 3,000-word doc."

She would not approve it for a broader audience in its current form. She would approve the playbook core (Phases 1-4, measurement, decision guide) if it were extracted and tightened.

---

## What would move this to an 8

1. Extract the market-by-market section, current rollout status, and MCC structure into a separate operational doc (or update Eyes). This removes roughly 900 words of ephemeral content from a durable playbook.
2. Consolidate the DE data presentation. Keep the table (it is powerful). Cut the narrative that restates the same numbers in prose above it.
3. Move the cross-reference links from the opening paragraph to the Related section or a brief callout after the first section.
4. Add a one-line Execution Guide cross-reference at the end of Phase 1, where the reader actually needs it.
5. The result: a roughly 1,800-word playbook that is tight, durable, forwardable, and earns every paragraph.
