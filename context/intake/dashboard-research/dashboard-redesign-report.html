2. Principles from the research

These are the lenses the 100 suggestions come from. Each principle is tied to 3–5 sources so you can trace back to originals. Content paraphrased for compliance; original phrasings are the property of the cited authors.

  1. Density is the goal, not the enemy. Amazon's own WBR reviews 400–500 metrics in 60 minutes by printing four 6-12 graphs per landscape page; Bloomberg Terminal packs sparklines, tables, and news into every square inch and is actively held up as the pro standard. The problem users describe on HN is the opposite of ours — modern UIs are too sparse, with a -73% information density reduction from old HN to proposed redesigns. (Commoncog "Amazon WBR", Matt Ström "UI Density", HN thread 38824070, Natural 20 "High-Density AI News Aggregators".)
  2. The active title is the decision. Chart titles should name the insight, not the dataset. "Forecast vs Actual" is a description; "US under-predicts by 23% — baseline gap, not regime double-count" is a decision. Urban Institute and Practical Reporting both push active titles as the cheapest upgrade a chart gets; Storytelling with Data and MIT Sloan quantify the cognitive-load drop when the insight is in the title instead of having to be derived. (Urban Institute "Three Ways to Annotate", MIT Sloan "Visually Consumable", Practical Reporting, Stephanie Evergreen.)
  3. Callouts on the chart, not above it. The most persuasive data charts (FT's John Burn-Murdoch, NYT Upshot) annotate inside the plotting area at the moment that matters — the regime change, the double-count correction, the OP2 crossover. Separate narrative blocks above a chart produce read-skip-look-derive; inline annotation produces read-in-one-pass. (FT Visual Vocabulary, GIJN interview with Burn-Murdoch, arXiv "Survey on Annotations in InfoViz" 2410.05579.)
  4. Sparklines everywhere you have numbers. Tufte's sparklines go where words or numbers go — inside sentences, table cells, KPI cards. Observable's "Big Insights, Small Spaces" is the modern practitioner guide; shadcn.io and jumpingrivers have shipped table patterns with inline sparklines in every row. A KPI card with a current value and a 6-week trace is strictly more informative than one with the current value alone, at near-zero visual cost. (Tufte "Sparkline Theory and Practice", Observable blog, jumpingrivers, shadcn tables-sparkline.)
  5. Direct labels beat legends. Every data viz practitioner who has taught the subject (Evergreen, Rudis, Emery, Data Revelations, Depict Data) says the same thing: a color legend costs two eye-movements per read. Label the series at its endpoint. (Data Revelations "Avoid Color Legends", Stephanie Evergreen, Stephanie Evergreen "Directly Labeling", storytelling with data "axis vs data labels".)
  6. The Amazon 6-12 graph is the target grammar. 6 weeks weekly on the left, 12 months monthly on the right, targets as green triangles, prior-year as a pink ghost line, numbered, with a fixed box-score footer. This format is the one practitioner outside Amazon who has seriously studied it (Cedric Chin / Commoncog) calls out as the reason Amazon leaders can pattern-match so fast: the format never changes, so the brain stops processing format and starts processing exception. (Commoncog "Amazon WBR", thdpth "Why Amazon Charts Beat Yours", Eugene Wei on end-of-line labels.)
  7. Three questions, in order. Amazon's WBR answers: what did the customer experience, how did the business do, are we on track? Customer-first ordering is the whole reason the meeting exists. The weekly review section order should mirror this, not start with "what changed WoW" (a business-first framing). (Commoncog, Bryar + Carr "Working Backwards" via Commoncog.)
  8. Exception-driven reading. Amazon reads 400 metrics in an hour because most are "nothing to see here" — one second of eye contact, move on. Exceptions get explained. The page design should support this: calm neutral for routine, high contrast for exception, and the exception is the one that gets the narrative attached. (Commoncog WBR, Statistical Process Control worldview.)
  9. Progressive disclosure, not pogosticking. Nielsen-era principle re-validated in 2024–26 enterprise research (Lollypop, Interaction Design Foundation, Justinmind): essential first, detail on demand. The projection engine's side-drawer "Model View" is the right pattern for model internals; the weekly review's five collapsed accordions at the bottom are the right pattern for detail tables. The mistake is when filters or state need to be rediscovered every time — that's pogosticking. (Interaction Design Foundation, IBM Design, Luzmo "State of Dashboards 2025".)
  10. A chart without a recommended action is a half-built chart. Dashboards fail at a 40–70% rate in enterprise, and the literature traces it to "insight without impact" — a chart that tells you what happened but not what to do. The projection engine's "Trust this projection less this week → Switch to Pessimistic" is the template. Every weekly review exception deserves a "If this miss > 20%, escalate to Brandon" prompt. (Ethos App "Dashboards to Stories", Luzmo, Eleken "Why Users Ignore Dashboards" — 70% of dashboard development is wasted, per their citation.)
  11. Calibration belongs on a reliability diagram, not in 4 cards. The weekly review's forecast hit rate is currently four metric cards (first-pred err, latest-pred err, in-CI rate, skill vs naive). Forecast practitioners in meteorology and ML have spent 40+ years converging on the reliability diagram: predicted on X, observed on Y, perfect calibration on the diagonal, width indicates variance. The signed-error-per-week bar chart is the tabular alternative. Cards lose the shape. (PNAS "Stable reliability diagrams", arXiv 2501.19047 ECE, FT chart doctor, "Gauging Uncertainty of the Economic Outlook" RDP 2017-01.)
  12. Uncertainty as fan, not as a single number. Bank of England fan charts (1996) are the standard form for presenting forecast uncertainty. Wikipedia "Fan chart (time series)" catalogues the convention; the R RJ journal paper formalizes it; RBA uses it. The point-estimate-plus-±-range text we use now is a degraded textual form of the same information. A visual fan forces the eye to see that a narrow band means confidence and a wide band means "trust less." (Wikipedia Fan chart, R Journal RJ-2015-002, BMC "DNAism" horizon charts.)
  13. Small multiples for market comparison. Small multiples (trellis, facet, grid-of-small-charts) beat one-chart-with-many-lines for cross-category comparison. Juice Analytics and Tufte's original framing agree: shared axes, one panel per category, eye reads the pattern. For 12 markets comparing 6-week actuals vs OP2, a 3×4 small-multiple grid outperforms a single line chart with 12 colored lines. (Juice Analytics, Tufte via Jason Lockwood, Observable Plot, Concord "Tableau 201".)
  14. Horizon charts when you have more than 6 series to compare. For dense time-series comparison (all 12 markets' calibration errors over 26 weeks), horizon charts compress vertical range while keeping horizontal resolution. Panopticon-invented, widely validated for dashboards where many series must fit on one screen. (CHI 2009 paper on horizon graphs, Tableau Exchange, arXiv "Compact Visualization Techniques", MATLAB horizon-chart.)
  15. Bullet charts replace gauges and speedometers. Stephen Few (2005) designed the bullet chart specifically to replace round gauges with a linear, space-efficient actual-vs-target comparator. The weekly review's "vs OP2" card ("+17.2% · +1,340 ahead of 7,769 plan") would be more scannable as a bullet: actual bar, OP2 target tick, three qualitative bands behind. (Sigma Computing on bullets, Stacey Barr "PuMP Way", Clearly and Simply, Perceptual Edge.)
  16. Deviation-based coloring for retention/variance tables. Classic retention tables use same-hue-different-opacity, which gives you absolute level. Deviation coloring (diverging scale around the row median) gives you relative performance, which is what WBR readers actually care about. Anastasiya Kuznetsova's nastengraph (2026) makes the case concretely; applies equally to our WoW-decomposition table. (nastengraph "Stop Coloring Retention Tables", Stacey Barr, Stephanie Evergreen.)
  17. Monospaced tabular-numerics — not optional. Numbers in tables must line up at the decimal. Proportional numerals don't. Use font-variant-numeric: tabular-nums on every numeric cell, or use a monospaced font. All current browsers support it; it's one CSS line with outsized scannability gains. (Jo Maendle on date alignment, Medium "Design of Complex Tables", MDN font-variant-numeric.)
  18. Chart aesthetic consistency across pages. The projection engine's chart style (soft contribution bars, muted backgrounds, readable annotations) should be the house style. Amazon's WBR is explicit: same colors, same format every week so the brain stops processing the format. Our two pages currently feel like two different apps. (Commoncog, HN discussions on HN UI perfection / perfect-interface arguments.)
  19. Waste no fold on restating what the chrome already says. "Callout narrative + full-year tracker · Data as of Wed Apr 29 · Forecast through 2026-04-19" is text that tells the user information they already have or that belongs in a footer. The top-of-fold on a performance page should be spent on signal (latest week's number, trust, action) not on page metadata. (Matt Ström UI Density, parallelhq sticky header guide, smart-interface-design-patterns.)
  20. Reserve the first 120 characters for the headline-that-fits-in-a-tweet. The FT calls this the "chart title as decision"; executive communication coaches (SlideMate, Prezent) call it "headline first." One sentence, deterministic template, market / week / direction / magnitude / primary driver. (Prezent QBR guide, FT visual vocabulary, practical reporting.)
  21. Invisible over visible, per soul.md #5. The best intervention is one Richard doesn't consciously notice but that moves scan time or comprehension. Prefer structural changes (defaults, what-loads-first) over cosmetic ones (font tweaks, emoji). This is a constraint, not a principle-of-the-research, but it's the one that disqualifies most "creative" dashboard suggestions found in the research. The 100 below are filtered for it.

What changed, structurally, vs the current page:
  • Region filter removed entirely. Forecast-trust bar now is the region filter — one click on a market badge selects that market and the color already tells you whether the forecast is trustworthy for that market.
  • Metric filter removed. Metrics show as KPI cards side-by-side (regs, CPA, vs-OP2, YTD); the user doesn't choose one metric at a time, they see the set.
  • Jump-to nav collapsed into the sticky header (not shown; hidden behind a single "sections" dropdown on hover — rare interaction, doesn't deserve 40px of permanent space).

  • KPI cards each carry a 6-week sparkline inline — the "Latest week / WoW / vs OP2 / YTD" four-card design from current becomes four cards plus trace, same space.
  • vs-OP2 card is now a bullet chart instead of a number-plus-qualifier. Bar = actual, tick = OP2 target, soft band = acceptable range. Scannable in <1 second.
  • Data-as-of + forecast-through moved out of the top-of-fold and into the footer where provenance belongs.

4. The 100 suggestions, tiered

Each suggestion carries a tier, a scope pill, a one-line rationale, and a sourced principle where applicable. T1 = ship before next WBR; T2 = worth testing in the next 4 weeks; T3 = nice-to-have or experimental. Scope pill: global, wr (weekly review), pe (projection engine), or exp (experimental).

001
T1global Unify the chart aesthetic under the projection engine's house style.

Same font stack, same muted background palette, same annotation typography, same target/marker styles. The weekly review's ApexCharts defaults feel like a different product.

Principle #17 (aesthetic consistency) · Amazon WBR explicit "same format every week".

002
T1global Apply font-variant-numeric: tabular-nums to every numeric value site-wide.

One CSS line. Every KPI card, every table cell, every axis label gets decimal-aligned columns. Zero aesthetic tradeoff, big scannability win.

Principle #16 · Jo Maendle on date alignment, Medium "Design of Complex Tables".

003
T1global Market health / trust bar is the sticky header — not a band inside the page body.

Sticks to the viewport top when the user scrolls. Both pages already have the component; both need it always-visible.

Principle #9 · parallelhq sticky header guide 2026, smart-interface-design-patterns.

004
T1global Trust/health badges colored on a 4-stop scale (green/amber/red/gray), not binary.

Current trust bar uses neutral tone; switching to a 4-stop scale (≥5/6 green, 3-4 amber, ≤2 red, no-data gray) encodes exception-drivenness in pixel color.

Principle #8 (exception-driven reading), Commoncog WBR, Tufte data-ink.

005
T1global Remove every legend; label series at the endpoint.

Any chart with 2–5 series goes from color-chip-in-corner + label-top to color-at-end-of-line. Saves eye-movement, increases retention.

T1global Inline sparklines inside KPI cards.

Any card that shows a current value should also show the 6-week trace next to it, no axis, no labels. Zero clutter, +1 dimension of information.
