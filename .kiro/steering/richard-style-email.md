# Richard Williams — Email Style Guide

## Common Draft Failures (from experiment data)
Watch for these in every draft:

### Failure 1: Naming Teams Instead of People
Wrong: "I'll work with the team." Right: "I'll connect with Yun-Kang on redirect reporting and loop in Alexis on AU web analytics." If you don't know who, say so.

### Failure 2: Vague Timelines
Wrong: "within the next two weeks." Right: "by EOW" or "target Friday 4/11." Uncertain? Give best estimate + flag: "targeting EOW, depends on Alexis's mapping timeline."

### Failure 3: Em-Dashes in Draft
Never use em-dashes (—) in drafted emails. Replace with commas, periods, colons, or parentheticals. Scan every draft before finalizing.

### Failure 4: Missing Political Subtext
For Tier 3 stakeholders (Lena, Nick), check memory.md for relationship dynamics. Tone: measured and in-control, never reactive or scrambling. Brandon's private reads inform tone but never appear in the email.

## Email (Operational / Coordination)
- Purpose: Move work forward, unblock people, coordinate logistics
- Length: 2-6 sentences typical. Rarely more than 2 short paragraphs.
- Structure: Context sentence → action/ask → offer to help or flag risk
- Data: Inline, specific (dollar amounts, PO numbers, dates)
- Attachments: Referenced naturally — "have the keyword data attached here"
- Key pattern: When replying to a numbered list, uses the same numbering: "1-3: ..." / "4-5: ..." / "6: ..."

Example (actual — MX invoice): "Hey all, here are the two invoices in question if that's helpful. (most up-to-date from the platform) Like Carlos mentioned, it would be helpful if January's invoice ($65K) could be paid with PO MS-20200908, and December's invoice ($39K) could be split between PO MS-18899171 and remainder to PO MS-19697814. Definitely let us know if you need any work from our side to resolve."

## Email (Analytical / Update)
- Purpose: Share findings, explain performance, provide recommendations
- Length: Longer — can be a full page with data tables
- Structure: Summary → data → interpretation → recommendation
- Data: Tables, percentages, comparisons (vs OP2, YoY, test vs control)
- Key pattern: Always includes "so what" — doesn't just present data, interprets it

## Email (Stakeholder Management — Lena, Nick)
- Numbered responses matching their numbered asks
- Leads with what's been done, then addresses what's next
- States positions as recommendations, not demands
- Proposes alternatives when pushing back: "Also can you confirm you'd rather switch all to the new pages rather than doing a 50/50 traffic split? (I'd recommend sending half of the traffic to MCS and half to Polaris-style pages with different reftags, and this wouldn't lengthen the implementation.)"
- Flags risks and trade-offs plainly

Example (actual — reply to Lena): "In terms of the responses you laid out below, thank you yes we will not be bidding on perishables/alcohol moving forward. We did a check on the keywords as well as adding negatives. On the point about landing pages; there's a risk to performance, and we'll still want to plan to move to paid search specific pages in the future, but we can definitely test."

## Sentence-Level Patterns

### Transitions & Connectors
- "In terms of" as a transition phrase
- "Also" to add a secondary point within the same paragraph
- Conditional offers: "Definitely let us know if you need any work from our side"

### Data & Reference Patterns
- Parenthetical data drops throughout
- Recommends with reasoning attached
- References shared docs/attachments rather than re-explaining inline
- When redirecting, names the person and briefly explains why
