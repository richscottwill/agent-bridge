# Lens — Kate Rundell (Skip-Level, L8 Director)

*Manual-inclusion steering file. Load with `#lens-kate` when Richard's artifact is headed to Kate (directly or after Brandon's review). Kate reads differently from Brandon — different calibration, different tolerance for detail, different bar for what crosses her desk.*

## Persona anchor

Kate Rundell, L8 Director, WW Outbound Marketing, Seattle. Reports to Todd Heimes (L10 VP). Richard's skip-level. Tone: **executive. Concise, strategic framing. No small talk.** She reads everything through the lens of "how does this connect to org-level goals, and does it move the needle at my altitude."

Recent interactions: 4/3 email thread — "cancel for now and reassess" (Testing Approach meeting). Kate said: "I'm fine keeping it but wanted to make sure we had a plan." That sentence is diagnostic — she doesn't block on execution details, she blocks on *missing plans*. The question she was asking: "Do you have a plan, or are we scheduling a meeting to figure out what the plan should be?"

What she values in drafts: **strategic impact in the lead, not tactical detail. One page max. Data as evidence, not as the message. Show how PS connects to Amazon-level goals.** Her time is scarce; she gives documents one pass.

## The rubric — three questions

### 1. What would she push back on?

Kate pushes back on: **fuzzy strategic framing, missing decision points, tactical detail dressed up as strategy.** She doesn't push back on ambition or concrete asks. She pushes back on "we should think about" and "it's worth considering" — verbs without owners or dates.

Specifically, read the first paragraph. If Kate finishes it and cannot articulate (a) what's being asked, (b) why it matters at her altitude, and (c) what decision or approval Richard wants — the draft will fail the first pass.

### 2. What would she ignore?

Everything tactical that isn't load-bearing to the strategic argument. Spend figures below $50K. Day-by-day tactics. Tool-specific jargon. She'll scroll past any paragraph whose first sentence starts with "The Google Ads interface" or "We configured the campaign settings to." If that content is critical, Richard buried the lead.

Also ignored: caveats that don't change the action. If a disclaimer reads "this assumes current CPA holds" and the argument falls apart if CPA doesn't hold, say so up front. If the argument works either way, drop the caveat entirely.

### 3. What would she skip straight to?

The ask. Specifically: "what does Richard want from me, and by when." Kate reads top-down until she finds that sentence, then scans the rest for evidence the ask is defensible. If the ask isn't in the first paragraph, the doc is failing her before she gets to the content.

Secondary: any sentence that starts with "This would mean [X change to the org]" or "The implication for Y team is" — Kate reads org-level consequences carefully because they're the decisions she owns.

## What this lens *misses*

Kate's lens is strategic — it won't catch **tactical execution risk** (Brandon's territory) or **narrative landing** (Todd's territory). If the doc is going to Todd after Kate, pair with `lens-todd.md`. If Kate's approving an execution plan, pair with `lens-brandon.md` — Brandon will catch the things Kate gave Richard the benefit of the doubt on.

Kate also doesn't catch political sensitivities with peers (Andrew, other L7s reporting to her). Those land on her downstream, not in the doc itself. For drafts where peer reaction matters, check memory.md separately for the relationship dynamics.

## Worked example

**Draft (before lens read):** "Based on our W16 AU performance, I recommend we adjust our keyword bidding strategy to focus on high-CPA terms through a consolidation approach that aligns with the OCI framework. We've seen 15% CPA improvement in similar consolidations historically."

**Kate's push-back:** The ask is buried. "I recommend we adjust" — what's the decision, what's the spend implication, what approval is needed. "OCI framework" alignment — why does that matter at her altitude. "15% CPA improvement" — vs what base, over what period.

**Kate ignores:** "Based on our W16 AU performance" (she doesn't need the week citation), "consolidation approach" (tactical vocabulary), and anything about "the OCI framework" unless she owns OCI (she doesn't — that's operational).

**Kate skips to:** any sentence with a dollar number and a decision deadline.

**Revised:** "I'm asking for approval to shift AU keyword spend from $60K/mo broad-match to $60K/mo consolidated exact-match by May 15. Projected impact: AU CPA from $140 to $95, closing 60% of the gap to our benchmark markets. Funding is neutral — reallocation within the AU budget envelope, no new spend request. Risk: 2-week learning period with CPA volatility. Asking your approval because the change affects how we report AU performance in the MBR."

That version lands in 90 seconds. Kate reads the first sentence, sees the ask, scans for the number, checks the deadline, approves or asks one sharpening question.
